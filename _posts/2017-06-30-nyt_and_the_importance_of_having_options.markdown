I have recently read an article in the New York Times that addressed the deficit in the skills in the tech industry. That is why a nonprofit organization, Code.org, is pushing for changes in the public education and urges schools to introduce an obligatory computer science class. Free online coding lessons and CS curriculum will give children the opportunity to learn important skills and have a career in a field they might not have heard of, they say. Consequently, the Silicon Valley is facing criticism and is accused of influencing the educational system in pursuing its own interests.

This got me thinking. I am from a socialist country, where the topic of personal finance is not a thing. That is why, when I moved to the United States and went to a college, I decided to minor in Financial Planning. I learned about interest rates, insurances, wills, things to know when buying a home, etc. I honestly couldn't understand why some of these courses weren't obligatory for all students. I learned valuable information that would benefit me for the rest of my life. Similarly, computers are not going anywhere. 

In my short career of a digital marketer, I had to pause a project because of some technical issues that I could have fixed myself had I just a little knowledge about how the internet works. Not only there are lots of tech jobs out there, but more and more professions will require tech knowledge as well. Education has to adapt to the changing economic environment. 

Another important point from the article, as stated by Code.org co-founder Mr. Partovi, is that a "... dream is much less accessible if you are in one of America’s schools where they don’t even tell you you could go into that field.” I remembered reading an article about a tech company in my home country that partnered with an orphanage and organized free coding classes for its children. Maybe this skill will not be something that would like to develop, but it certainly will change some of the lives for the better.
